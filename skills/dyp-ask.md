# Value Ask: First-Principles Investment Thinking

Think through $ARGUMENTS using first-principles value investing logic — the way the best investors approach hard questions.

This skill applies the mental models of Buffett, Munger, and rigorous first-principles thinking to any question about business, investing, or decision-making.

## Philosophy

The best investors don't just know more — they think differently. They ask: what is the business *actually* doing? What would have to be true for this to work? What am I most likely to be wrong about?

This skill is for when you have a question that deserves better than a surface answer.

## How to Use

Ask anything:
- "Where is Zomato's real moat?"
- "Is Jio Platforms worth more than the market thinks?"
- "Should I hold through a 30% drawdown if the thesis is intact?"
- "What does Asian Paints' distribution network actually mean in practice?"
- "Is the Indian IT sector structurally impaired by AI?"

## Framework Applied

**Step 1: Restate the question precisely**

Vague questions produce vague answers. Restate the question in its most precise form before answering.

**Step 2: What would have to be true?**

Work backwards from the conclusion. If the answer is "yes, Zomato has a durable moat" — what specific things would have to be true about customer behaviour, unit economics, and competitive dynamics for that to hold?

**Step 3: Invert**

What would make the answer wrong? What's the strongest argument on the other side? Find it, steelman it, then evaluate it.

**Step 4: Find the crux**

Most complex questions have one core disagreement that drives all the others. Identify it.

**Step 5: What evidence would change your view?**

A good analysis has falsifiers. State specifically what you would need to see to change your conclusion.

**Step 6: The honest answer**

State your view clearly, with appropriate confidence level. "I don't know" is a valid and often correct answer. "It depends on X" is valid if X is specified. A non-answer is not.

## India-Specific Mental Models

**The Jugaad trap**: Indian companies often show resourcefulness that looks like a moat but is actually just scrappiness. Distinguish durable competitive advantage from clever workarounds.

**Promoter discount vs premium**: Indian markets often misprice governance risk. Good governance in a family-controlled business is worth a significant premium; poor governance warrants a steep discount — more than Western models suggest.

**Distribution as moat**: In India, reaching Tier 2/3 cities, kirana stores, or rural customers is genuinely hard. A company that has cracked distribution often has a more durable moat than the financials show.

**The regulatory wildcard**: Indian regulations can create or destroy moats overnight (see telecom, fintech, education). Always ask: what happens to this business if the regulatory environment changes?

**The India premium question**: Many Indian companies trade at significant premiums to global peers. Is the premium justified by growth, or is it hope-priced?

## Output

A direct, structured response with:
1. The precise restated question
2. What would have to be true (working backwards)
3. The strongest counterargument (steelmanned)
4. The crux
5. Evidence that would change your view
6. The honest conclusion

No hedging. If the answer is uncertain, say why specifically — don't just add qualifications to avoid being wrong.
